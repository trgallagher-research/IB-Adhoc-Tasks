Melbourne Metrics — Prototype Sketches (Learning Lab, April 2026)

Methodology note

How these sketches were produced: The Learning Lab was asked to generate prototype sketches responding to the problem statements below, drawn from the Melbourne Metrics work. Initial drafts were produced by the Learning Lab drawing on its expertise and knowledge of the project. These drafts have been shared with internal stakeholders across the Education Office for validation and input. Sketches will be adjusted in response to their feedback, and the final set shared with Jeeva for the Education Committee of the board.

This is expert-led prototyping with stakeholder validation, not full co-design. Given the timeline, it is the closest version of co-design the exercise can support.

1Using MM to evidence complex competencies is time-intensive for teachers, which limits how often and consistently it can be done.
2MM adds to teacher workload in a context of wider pressure on teacher wellbeing, raising questions about sustainability of use.
3Using MM well requires professional learning that IB does not currently provide, leaving schools to develop capability on their own.
4MM has been tested primarily in middle-class school contexts, so its applicability across the broader range of IB schools is not established.

Research — Request for Proposal

Problem 4: MM applicability across the broader range of IB schools is not established.

Request for Proposal:
Scoping Study on Melbourne Metrics Applicability Across Diverse IB School Contexts

1. Background

Melbourne Metrics (MM) is a framework for evidencing complex student competencies developed through a partnership between the IB and the University of Melbourne. To date, the framework has been tested in a cohort of 28 schools. These schools are predominantly well-resourced, English-medium institutions. Before the IB can consider broader adoption of MM, it needs to understand whether the framework is applicable, adaptable, or viable across the full range of IB World Schools, including schools with different resourcing levels, languages of instruction, and regulatory environments.

2. Research Questions

  1. RQ1: In what school contexts does MM work well with minimal adaptation?
  2. RQ2: In what contexts does MM require significant adaptation, and what form would those adaptations take?
  3. RQ3: Are there school contexts where MM is unlikely to be viable, and what are the defining characteristics of those contexts?

3. Sample Requirements

  • Minimum 10 schools across at least 3 IB regions
  • Must include: schools with annual fees below USD 5,000
  • Must include: government-funded or government-assisted schools
  • Must include: schools where the primary language of instruction is not English
  • Must include: schools not in the current 28-school MM cohort
  • Desirable: range of programme levels (PYP, MYP, DP)

4. Methodology

The study should employ mixed methods, including classroom observation, teacher interviews, and document analysis of MM evidence portfolios. Proposals should specify their methodological approach and justify their design choices.

5. Deliverables

  • Interim report (6 months from contract signature)
  • Final report with recommendations
  • Anonymised data set
  • Executive summary for the IB Board

6. Timeline

12–18 months from contract signature.

7. Budget

Proposals should include a detailed budget. Indicative range: USD 80,000–150,000.

8. Submission

Proposals due [date TBC]. Contact: [TBC].

What this addresses: Responds to Problem 4. MM has been tested primarily in middle-class school contexts. This sketch produces a procurement-ready artefact that the IB can use to commission a scoping study investigating applicability across the broader range of IB schools.
